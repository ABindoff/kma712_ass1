  1. Using NCBI's BLAST, find out what species and gene this sequence is from (if you can... I kind of edited it a bit). Report the results of your search, with all the relevant statistics that are returned, and explain what each means.  (E.value, percent identity, query cover etc.)
  2. Make a selection of related sequences of different species: five hits that have the best possible E-value; five other hits in the 90-99% identity range; and five other hits with lower identity. This should give you 15 sequences. Download the complete gene if possible.
  3. Align the sequences using SeaView and include your alignment in the results; then use  muscle and save the output as a .NEXUS file, for input to phylogenetic analysis programs. Comment on any differences between the two alignments.
  4. Build a Neighbour-Joining tree using any program you choose (cite which one; e.g., SplitsTree) for your alignment and estimate bootstrap values.
  5. Estimate the most parsimonious tree for the sequences using any program you choose (cite which one), and save it. Also do a bootstrap analysis with at least 100 replicates to estimate the robustness of the tree you find.
  6. Using IQTree (which is available in BioLinux), find the likelihood model that best fits your alignment and find the most likely tree using that model. Detail all the relevant outputs.
  7. Finally, use SplitsTree and HKY distances to display a phylogenetic network for your data.
  8. Describe and report all phylogenetic estimations; remark on areas of uncertainty and of robustness, and anything else that appears interesting about your findings.
  9. EXTENSION, if you wish: Find another gene for these species and compare your trees...
